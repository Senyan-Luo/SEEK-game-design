Senyan led the team in the design process, facilitated station 2 during playtesting, and built this website to document the process.

My research paper examines how and why serious games should use the four pillars of learning as a guiding design framework. Although our simulation is not a digital game, it connects with serious games well because they share the common objective of educating players on a given topic. I consider Turn-it-in relay race a physical serious game that simulates how students manage their time and balance between life and academic responsibilities. Moreover, our relay race is an abstraction of a real struggle every student knows - finishing a research paper on time. In the simulation, participants will learn and experience the consequences of their choices as well as its impact on their team’s overall performance.

Elijah contributed to the design process, facilitated station 1 during playtesting, and collaborated on the designer statement.

My research paper is on the topic of immersion in videogames. Although our game is not a videogame, immersion is still very present. In the creation of any game with a narrative, and especially in a simulation, immersion is vital in making the experience engaging or believable. Our game is meant to simulate a student’s lifestyle choices when under stress. The players had to make decision, each choice having advantages and disadvantages, that reflected the choices made by students under a deadline. The gameplay advantages and disadvantages had to reflect the real life consequences of these decisions or else the players’ immersion would be broken and our message would be unclear. Without the basic principles of immersion, no one would take our game seriously as a simulation.

My research paper is about how video gaming can have positive effects on kids with ASD. It has very little to do with our simulation design but I think they both share come core concepts. For example, they both express the importance of motor, cognitive, and social skills. In my paper, I explain how video games can aid in the overall performance of kids who typically lack in those areas. In our simulation, motor skills are vital in order to win because in order to win, the players have to balance the plate and run at the same time. Additionally, in both my paper and our simulation, there is a stress on communication. For many kids with autism, communication and social skills are always difficult to maintain and control. Video games can create virtual relationships with these kids to help with that. In our simulation, communication is vital because each team needs to know when to run and when to pass the plate.

The Design Process

Ideation

In our first brainstorming session, we knew we wanted to create a game that can simulate some aspect of college life. Several great ideas were generated during the conversation and we came to a consensus: we want to address the stress college students experience. In the following meeting, Senyan proposed a relay race that would simulate students who are trying to meet a deadline to turn in their research paper, and each participant will make independent choices that will decide how they run in the race. Our team decided to build upon this simulaiton proposal. We used a whiteboard to collaboratively brainstorm on the game design.

A whiteboard snapshot that documents our ideation process. A, B, C and D represent four questions that require corresponding racer on a team to make a choice. Every choice comes with its unique reward (+) and punishment (-).

Pilot playtesting & Feedback

Beside logistics, our main goal of these two playtesting rounds is test the design of our rules. Specifically, we need to know how well the questions and the options work. Does any rule confuse the participants? Will they lean towards certain choices? Are these questions successful at simulating how students make choices under stress?
We playtested twice with peers from our discussion section and gained valuable insights on design logistics that needed improvements.

Based on the feedback, we identified some main problems and proposed the following changes:

  • We gathered all the participants and went over the specific directions and rules of the game. However, many participants forgot the questions they needed to answer after they are sent to their respective stations.

    To address this problem, we will announce only the general rules and purpose of the simulaiton at the beginning. Then we send the runners to their assigned station, where they will hear about the specific question that they need to answer from the designated facilitator at the station.

  • We took too long to get the players ready. In addition, Senyan forgot to bring the weighted backpacks to station 2, and Kook forgot to put the tennis ball on one of the player's plate.

    To keep things organized, we will set up all the materials at each station prior to game start, and once the players made their choices, we will gear them up and prepare them for their turn.

  • A player passed the plate to someone who is on the opposite team! This happened because he did not remember who were on his team.

    To prevent this from happening, we will line up two teams on two seperate sides, and give everyone a sticky badge to write either "A" or "B" to indicate team.

  • Participants from two teams inadventently made the same choice on every question. This meant that the options were unbalanced and people chose options that had no time punishment.

    First, we will extend the running distance. Since our initial distance was set at 30 feet per racer, even a 3-second time punishment is too significant. We will increase the distance per racer to 160 feet.
    In addition, we will tweak the duration of time punishment in every option and introduce more balance, so that participants will not lean toward one option without much thinking.

The Game

Final Playtesting

 
Duration and Players

On average, one round of relay race takes about 10 minutes. For playtesting, we completed 3 rounds, with 2 teams of 4 in each round

Props

2 identical plates, each filled with 2 books
2 research papers
8 pencils
4 backpacks (15 lbs each) filled with books
2 tennis balls
8 adhesive team tags

General Instructions

The main point of the game is to simulate how college students make choices regarding sleep, food, exercise, and most importantly, time management during stressful periods. The choices each participant makes will result in the consequences of their actions and determine their overall performance in the game. The goal is to turn in your research paper to your professor while managing important aspects of your daily life. You will be broken off into two groups of 4, and each group will assign number 1 through 4 to every participant. This is the order that you will go. Number 1 from both team will report to station 1, and so on. Both stations 1 and 3 will be positioned at one end, and stations 2 and 4 will be positioned at the other end. The plate acts as the baton of the relay race. As each person travels to the next station, they will have to balance a plate with different objects, because as life gets harder, there is a lot on everybody’s plate (wink wink). The objective is to finish the game as quickly as possible, while making conscientious decisions about your life. Whoever finishes first is the winner.
